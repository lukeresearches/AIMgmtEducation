Overview

A comprehensive pathway for AI integration in management education

This document outlines a pathway for integrating foundational AI courses with Dalhousie's management programs to build practical AI literacy for BComm, BMgmt, and MBA students. Informed by an initial conversation with Dr. Hamed Aghakhani and research and experience in AI-enabled management education, it proposes program requirements, a course design process, key deliverables, and initial curriculum and pedagogy ideas.

The courses would use an evidence-based, experiential approach that moves from core AI concepts to applied practice in management, finance, and organizational contexts, supporting students in building artifacts that will support their management careers. The outline is intended as a starting point, to be refined into a fully designed curriculum, pedagogy, and built-in evaluation on AI readiness for the courses.

Theory of Value

Our vision for AI-enabled management education

Integrating AI foundations courses with Dalhousie's world-class management education will provide undergrad and MBA students practical AI literacy that amplifies how they learn, work, and lead in AI-enabled organizations.

12-Week Curriculum

Progressive journey from foundations to advanced applications (click cards to expand)

Weeks 1-3: AI Foundations
Week 1 ‚ñº

AI Disruption & Literacy Foundations

AI overview; generative vs. predictive AI; models, prompts, context; AI innovation; adoption; ethics (bias, privacy, climate); implications for early careers, course roadmap.

Application: Complete an "AI impact scan" of risks, opportunities, open questions for their industry/function aided by transparent, structured AI use.
Week 2 ‚ñº

Co-Intelligence: AI as a Learning Partner & Mindset

AI as a co-pilot. Tools for learning (e.g. ChatGPT/Study); iterative and complex prompt-crafting (e.g. roles, chain-of-thought, system instructions); human in the loop; academic integrity and critical reflection.

Application: Building on Week 1, students use iterative prompts to critique and reinforce AI impact scan.
Week 3 ‚ñº

AI in Business, Finance & Entrepreneurship

Cross-functional use cases in marketing, operations, HR, finance, and entrepreneurship; AI in forecasting / decision support; AI-enabled business models of main labs (e.g. OpenAI), and AI firms (e.g., CaseText's valuation jump).

Application: Students use impact scan to propose an AI enhancement focus problem.
Weeks 4-6: AI in Practice
Week 4 ‚ñº

AI Value Propositions and Research

AI value creation in organizations; decision and workflow automation; linking AI capabilities to customer problems.

Application: Students complete AI-assisted secondary research; customer research (simulated), and qualitative data analysis to begin articulating an AI value proposition with model inputs and outputs.
Week 5 ‚ñº

AI Analysis / Prototyping

Students learn the basics of AI-assisted coding and no-code tools, including spreadsheet-based analysis.

Application: Students develop initial logic model for value proposition, and prototype with no-code solutions.
Week 6 ‚ñº

AI in Organizations: Leadership & Collaboration

AI-enabled workflows and roles; collaborating with AI as a teammate; change management; communication with stakeholders; equity and inclusion considerations in AI adoption; preparing for ongoing AI change.

Application: Students present redesigned workflow highlighting benefits, costs, and leadership asks to mock boards from peers. AI board member prompted to critique plan on economics, ethics, and hallucination.
Weeks 7-9: AI Strategy and Governance
Week 7 ‚ñº

AI Governance and Regulation

Managing bias and fairness; data privacy (e.g., GDPR, Canada's regulation evolution); IP and copyright issues; the role of the manager in establishing AI guardrails.

Application: Students identify / respond to compliance risks to their proposed business case and develop a leadership memo for managing such risk.
Week 8 ‚ñº

AI Strategy: Building a Roadmap for RoI

Rethinking organizational, technological, and design systems to leverage AI capabilities. Moves beyond one-off AI tools to creating adaptive, intelligent, and human-centric systems that provide Return on Investment.

Application: Students develop AI strategy and implementation roadmap for target organization.
Week 9 ‚ñº

The Future of Work: Human-AI Collaboration

Advanced models of human-AI teaming; upskilling and reskilling the workforce; ethical considerations in automated decision-making; the manager's role as an AI orchestrator.

Application: Informed by case studies, students design a training program and change management plan for their focal organization.
Weeks 10-12: Advanced Application Sprint
Week 10 ‚ñº

Deep Dive: AI in Marketing & Customer Experience (CX)

Generative AI for content creation and hyper-personalization; predictive analytics for customer segmentation; AI-driven sales and service automation; ethical marketing.

Application: Teams use GenAI tools to develop a personalized marketing campaign and measure its simulated impact.
Week 11 ‚ñº

Deep Dive: AI Area of Choice

Students select an AI application area relevant to their career or academic focus (e.g., AI in Healthcare, Fintech, Sustainability, Robotics, Operations & Supply Chain, etc.). Focus on in-depth analysis of a high-value use case within that area, including market size, competitive landscape, technical feasibility, and ethical considerations.

Application: Refine business case with greater insights from research.
Week 12 ‚ñº

Final Synthesis & Portfolio Presentation

Students select a high-potential project and develop a Go-to-Market Artifact designed to attract a specific audience. Sample options: 1) White Paper, or Podcast (cited for a target company/industry), 2) Prototype & Landing Page (low-fidelity prototype and persuasive landing page to test market interest), or 3) "AI-Ready" Portfolio (curated presentation of course artifacts framed as a value proposition for specific role).

Application: Final portfolio presentation showcasing AI competencies developed throughout the course.

Course Design Process

Strategic approach to curriculum development

1. Clarify vision and constraints

Meet with FoM leadership (MBA, BComm, BMgmt) to define goals, timelines, and how the courses anchor future AI offerings and certificates.

2. Draft high-level course outline

Use existing knowledge, and the above requirements to sketch a shared outline (modules, core concepts, assessment approach) for courses.

3. Map to existing curriculum

Scan core course outlines and meet with key instructors to align the draft outline with current objectives, assignments, and pathways.

4. Ground in evidence and market needs

Refresh recent literature reviews on AI in management education and consult employers/industry partners to refine outcomes.

5. Design modular, updatable building blocks

Turn the outline into reusable modules that support undergrad and MBA, and can be updated as tools change.

6. Pilot, measure, and refine

Test selected weeks or modules in existing settings (e.g., PPE, guest sessions), collect quick AI-readiness and engagement data plus faculty feedback.

7. Complete week-by-week syllabus

Translate the module framework into detailed, week-by-week syllabus (topics, readings, in-class labs, assessments, and example AI tools).

8. Prepare for scalable delivery and evolution

Package teaching guides, assessment rubrics, and tech setup so other instructors can deliver the courses, and define how to refresh tools, cases, and examples.
